Solving Problems#

Given the focus of data science coursework on the math, statistics, and programming that is required to use data science tools, one would be forgiven for thinking that understanding these tools is the ultimate goal of data science.

At the end of the day, however, your success as a data scientist will not be evaluated by whether you write good code or whether your classification model‚Äôs AUC score is high. No, your success as a data scientist will always be evaluated based on a much simpler criterion: did you make your stakeholder‚Äôs life better by solving a problem they faced?

In the introductory chapter of this book, I said that that one of the key goals of this book is to help young data scientists understand the broader context of their work. There is perhaps no better way to do that then to always keep this one fact front of mind. The things you practice in class exercises ‚Äî writing good code and finding a classification model with a high AUC ‚Äî are things that may help you achieve the goal of solving your stakeholder‚Äôs problem, but they are never sufficient in and of themselves. Indeed, it is hard to overstate how common it is to see talented young data scientists write brilliant, performant code and fit extremely accurate models that predict the wrong property, solve a problem that isn‚Äôt actually core to the stakeholder‚Äôs needs, require data not available in deployment, or work in training but won‚Äôt generalize to the stakeholder‚Äôs deployment context. The data scientists on these projects often deployed the skills they learned in technical classes magnificently, but this execution was not well-directed in service of the stakeholder ‚Äî the classic ‚Äúnot seeing the forest for the trees‚Äù mistake.

Note

Throughout this book, I will frequently use the term ‚Äústakeholder‚Äù to refer to the person whose problem that you, the data scientist, is seeking to address. I use this term because, as a young data scientist, you will often be in the position of having to use your data science skills to help someone else. Thus your stakeholder may be your manager, your CEO, or someone at another company you are advising.

However, if you‚Äôre lucky enough to not be directly answerable to someone else, either because you work for yourself or because you‚Äôre in a field that gives you substantial autonomy like academia, you can simply think of your ‚Äústakeholder‚Äù as yourself.

If you‚Äôre interested in developing a consumer-facing product (e.g., you‚Äôre an independing developer whose thinking of creating a new data-science-based web app), you may also find it useful to think of your customer of the stakeholder, since very few products are successful if they don‚Äôt solve a problem customers face.

Solving the Right Problem#

How, then, can we be sure our hard work as data scientists serves the interests of our stakeholder?

The first step in solving any problem is always to carefully specify the problem. While this may seem obvious, properly articulating the core problem one seeks to address can be remarkably difficult. Moreover, because everything you will do after articulating your problem is premised on having correctly specified your objective, it is the greatest determinant of the success of your project. The most sophisticated, efficiently executed, high precision, high recall model in the world isn‚Äôt worth a lick of good if the results it generates don‚Äôt solve the problem you or your stakeholder need solved.

Specifying your problem not only ensures that your subsequent efforts are properly directed, but it can also radically simplify your task. Many times problems only appear difficult because of how they are presented. As Charles Kettering, Head of Research at General Motors from 1920 to 1947 once said, ‚ÄúA problem well stated is a problem half solved.‚Äù

How do you know if you‚Äôve ‚Äúclearly articulated the problem,‚Äù and how should you go about refining your problem statement with your stakeholder? That is the topic of this and the following Chapters in Part I, as are strategies for using data to help inform this process through iterative refinement of your understanding of the contours of the problem space.

This chapter will be written as if you are the sole actor on a data science project, responsible for everything from problem articulation to execution. But of course, this will rarely be the case in your professional life as a data scientist, especially early in your career. With that in mind, in the next chapter we will turn to the topic of ‚ÄúStakeholder Management‚Äù ‚Äî the practice of refining your understanding of the problem to be solved with a stakeholder.

What is a Meaningful Problem?#

A meaningful problem is something your stakeholder is willing to allocate time and money to making different. If your stakeholder is a business, their problem may be that they are losing customers to a competitor and they don‚Äôt know how to prevent it. If your stakeholder is a hospital owner, their problem may be that they are regularly being sued when patients fall and get hurt and they would like to find a way to reduce the frequency of falls and lawsuits. Or if your stakeholder is a regulator, then their problem may be that they are uncertain whether authorizing a new drug would cause more harm than benefit and they want to reduce that uncertainty before making a problem.

Regardless of the context, though, a meaningful problem is something your stakeholder wants to be different about the world, and which they are willing to put time, effort, and resources into changing. This last part ‚Äî the willingness to put time, effort, and resources into addressing the problem ‚Äî is how we know that a given problem isn‚Äôt just ‚Äúsomething that‚Äôd be nice,‚Äù but is actually something that matters, and is thus something worth you putting your energy into address. Solving the problem of ‚Äúno one cleans their dirty mugs in the office sink‚Äù may get you a ‚Äúgood job‚Äù in the weekly staff meeting, but it‚Äôs not a problem whose solution is going to make your stakeholder‚Äôs life markedly better and get you promoted. And so before you dive into solving a problem, you should always stop to make sure it‚Äôs something that matters enough that, if told the problem could be solved with sufficient investment, your stakeholder would be willing to put in that investment.

Sometimes, a stakeholder problem will clearly indicate what the remedy will look like ‚Äî a government regulator charged with deciding whether a drug should be made publically available who isn‚Äôt sure about the consequences of authorization would be‚Ä¶ wants to know the consequences of drug authorization. But this will not always be the case ‚Äî often it‚Äôs easy to identify a problem (‚Äúwe‚Äôre losing customer‚Äôs to Bob‚Äôs Burgers down the street!‚Äù) in a way that doesn‚Äôt immediately imply what form a solution would take. And other times, as we‚Äôll discuss below, your stakeholder will assume they know what form a solution would take, and part of your job will be to differentiate between the actual problem (that you do need to address) and the assumed solution (which you should question, because it may be the best way to resolve the problem, but also may not).

If You Don‚Äôt Articulate The Problem#

There are many examples in the world of data science of projects going awry because the team behind them failed to properly articulate the problem they wished to solve. Rather than start with one of those true stories, however, I‚Äôd like to begin with one of my favorite fictional (and humorous) examples of the phenomenon.

In Douglas Adams‚Äô comedic sci-fi classic Hitchhiker‚Äôs Guide to the Galaxy, a race of hyperintelligent pandimensional beings set out to build a massive supercomputer the size of a city to solve the mysteries of the cosmos once and for all. When they turned on the computer, named Deep Thought, they announced that:

‚ÄúThe task we have designed you to perform is this. We want you to tell us‚Ä¶ the Answer!‚Äù

‚ÄúThe Answer?‚Äù said Deep Thought.

‚ÄúThe Answer to what?‚Äù

‚ÄúLife!‚Äù urged one designer.

‚ÄúThe Universe!‚Äù said another.

‚ÄúEverything!‚Äù they said in chorus.

Deep Thought paused, then answered, ‚ÄúLife, the Universe, and Everything. There is an answer. But,‚Äù Deep Thought added, ‚ÄúI‚Äôll have to think about it.‚Äù

Seven and a half million years later, when Deep Thought had finally finished its calculations, the descendants of those designers assembled to learn the result of their ancestors‚Äô work.

‚ÄúEr ‚Ä¶good morning, O Deep Thought,‚Äù said Loonquawl [one descendant] nervously, ‚Äúdo you have ‚Ä¶ er, that is ‚Ä¶‚Äù

‚ÄúAn answer for you?‚Äù interrupted Deep Thought majestically. ‚ÄúYes. I have.‚Äù

The two [descendants] shivered with expectancy. Their waiting had not been in vain.

‚ÄúThere really is one?‚Äù breathed Phouchg [the other descendant].

‚ÄúThere really is one,‚Äù confirmed Deep Thought.

‚ÄúTo Everything? To the great Question of Life, the Universe and Everything?‚Äù

‚ÄúYes. [‚Ä¶] Though I don‚Äôt think,‚Äù added Deep Thought, ‚Äúthat you‚Äôre going to like it.‚Äù

[‚Ä¶]

‚ÄúAll right,‚Äù said the computer, and settled into silence again.

The two fidgeted.

The tension was unbearable.

‚ÄúForty-two,‚Äù said Deep Thought, with infinite majesty and calm.

‚ÄúForty-two!‚Äù yelled Loonquawl. ‚ÄúIs that all you‚Äôve got to show for seven and a half million years‚Äô work?‚Äù

‚ÄúI checked it very thoroughly,‚Äù said the computer, ‚Äúand that quite definitely is the answer. I think the problem, to be quite honest with you, is that you‚Äôve never actually known what the question is.‚Äù

‚ÄúBut it was the Great Question! The Ultimate Question of Life, the Universe and Everything,‚Äù howled Loonquawl.

‚ÄúYes,‚Äù said Deep Thought with the air of one who suffers fools gladly, ‚Äúbut what actually is it?‚Äù

A slow stupefied silence crept over the men as they stared at the computer and then at each other.

‚ÄúWell, you know, it‚Äôs just Everything ‚Ä¶ everything ‚Ä¶‚Äù offered Phouchg weakly.

‚ÄúExactly!‚Äù said Deep Thought. ‚ÄúSo once you do know what the question actually is, you‚Äôll know what the answer means.‚Äù[1]

In addition to establishing the premise for one of the greatest comedic science fiction novels in human history,[2] I feel this passage perfectly exemplifies the three reasons most data science projects fail.

The first is our absolute faith that technology will save us. All we have to do to solve any problem is feed it into the newest, shiniest AI/LLM/ML model available. Sure, there may be technical challenges associated with configuring the environment, formatting the data, etc., but fundamentally, all that lies between us and success is putting the problem into technology‚Äôs hands.

The second is that when a data science project fails, it‚Äôs rarely because the technology itself failed. Rather, projects usually fail because people failed to ensure that the task they asked their model to accomplish would actually solve their problem. Technology doesn‚Äôt care if the task it‚Äôs been given is useful to the user. It will do what it has been asked to do ‚Äî no more and no less. Garbage in, garbage out.

Finally, this passage also gives a nod to the third ‚Äî and perhaps most important reason ‚Äî that data science projects fail: data scientists are far too deferential to their stakeholders. In this passage, we can see that Deep Thought recognizes the idiocy of the request it has been given by its stakeholders ‚Äî Loonqual and Phouchg ‚Äî but does nothing about it. And to the degree to which we can think of the Deep Thought personality as a stand-in for the data scientist, this is a troubling realistic illustration of how many data science interactions go. The stakeholder says they want something and the (usually younger) data scientist assumes their only responsibility is to ensure the stakeholder‚Äôs request is implemented.

But as discussed in the introduction of this book, your success as a data scientist will always be evaluated in terms of whether you‚Äôve made your stakeholder‚Äôs life better. And if doing precisely what they ask does not improve their lives, that‚Äôs the only thing that will matter. No one is blaming Deep Thought in this story, but they sure aren‚Äôt excited about what it did, either.

Articulating and Reframing Your Problem#

The preceding story gives an (admittedly extreme) example of how things can go wrong when a data science project is not well motivated ‚Äî in short, large amounts of energy and sweat can go into creating technically impressive results that, in the end, in no way solve a problem or improve the lives of anyone involved.

How, then, can you avoid that fate? Every problem is different, but here are some strategies to employ when faced with a problem to aid in refinement, reframing, and articulation. As we work through these, we will also work a few examples based on real cases designed to help you get a feel for what this process entails and why it‚Äôs so important.

1. How do you know if you‚Äôve been successful?#

One of the most helpful questions to ask when trying to understand a problem is ‚Äúhow would we know if we‚Äôve successfully solved this problem?‚Äù This question is powerful because it abstracts away the question of how you might solve a problem, and instead focuses attention on the goal one is trying to achieve. In other words, the objective of this question is to figure out how you might measure success.

Crucially, the answer to this question shouldn‚Äôt be something like ‚Äúwe have a good model for doing X‚Äù ‚Äî that answer has largely just shifted the question to what constitutes ‚Äúgood.‚Äù Rather, try to come up with an answer in terms of the metric or behavior that, if observed, would tell you that you‚Äôve been successful.

To illustrate the potential power of this question, let‚Äôs consider an example I‚Äôve encountered in the real world (with some details changed to protect the anonymity of everyone involved).

The stakeholder is an online auction site for used construction equipment (front loaders, backhoes, etc.). They want to improve the algorithm that suggests prices to people selling equipment, but they‚Äôve realized that while they have good data on how machinery type impacts prices, their algorithm can‚Äôt take into account the condition of equipment being listed. So they‚Äôve hired a data science team to train an image classification model on listings to estimate the condition of each listed piece of machinery.

So: how would you know if you‚Äôve been successful in solving this stakeholder‚Äôs problem?

At first blush, you might say I would know I was successful if I have a model that has high classification accuracy for equipment condition when fed photos of equipment listed in the past.

But look more carefully, is that really what the stakeholder cares about? No ‚Äî because ‚Äúimage classification model‚Äù is something young data scientists are familiar with, they tend to latch on to it immediately. ‚ÄúOh, great, image classification! I can do that. Let‚Äôs go!‚Äù

No, the problem motivating the stakeholder is that their price suggestion algorithm doesn‚Äôt take into account the condition of equipment going up for sale. Given that, you would know your model was successful if the predicted prices from the model were more in line with final sale prices for equipment of all conditions. That‚Äôs the actual goal.

Yes, an image classification model might allow the stakeholder to estimate equipment condition which could then be used as training data to revise the pricing model. But is that the best approach?

To train a model that can differentiate construction machinery in good or bad condition, the first thing you‚Äôd need to do is have a person go through and label a lot of old listings as being in good or bad condition. And you‚Äôd probably need a lot of them ‚Äî after all, your model would have to be able to differentiate between indicators of wear that are superficial (dirt, superficial rust, and peeling paint) and indicators of wear that are substantial (cracks, patch welds, etc.), and do so on a range of different types of construction equipment. Then you‚Äôd use that to train an image classifier, then you‚Äôd have to tune and validate that image classifier, then you could use its classifications to improve the pricing model.

Alternatively, you could just (a) have someone label some old listings and use those labels directly to refine the pricing model, and (b) add a drop-down menu that asks people listing equipment to report the condition of their equipment (so that data is available to the pricing model when someone goes to list a product). Congratulations! You‚Äôve just entirely removed image classification from this task.

2. Abstract the Problem#

A second strategy for better understanding a problem is to try to reframe it at a higher level of generality/abstraction. When you do, you may find your thorny problem is actually just a specific example of a more general type of problem, one that people smarter than either of us have already figured out how to solve.

Alternatively, you may realize that you or your stakeholder has (often unknowingly) introduced constraints to the problem that aren‚Äôt actually constraints.

Perhaps my favorite example of this phenomenon comes from a talk given by Vincent Warmerdam at PyData 2019.

The World Food Program (WFP) is a global leader in food aid provision. As Vincent tells the story ‚Äî which he reports having heard at an Operations Research Conference ‚Äî the WFP was struggling with an extremely difficult data science problem: how best to get food from the places it was being grown/stored to the people who needed it most. Essentially, the WFP would receive reports of needs from communities facing food insecurity. One community might report a need for bread and beef, while another might request lentils and chicken. The WFP would compile these requests and then set about trying to determine the most efficient way to meet these needs.

This type of logistics problem is an example of a notoriously difficult problem (essentially a version of the Traveling Salesman Problem, which is NP-Complete, if that means anything to you) that companies like FedEx and UPS buy supercomputers to address. But this particular problem was made extra challenging by all the different types of food the WFP was trying to provide communities.

What the WFP realized was that they didn‚Äôt actually need to provide bread to the village asking for bread. See, humans don‚Äôt need bread to avoid starvation ‚Äî they need a certain number of calories, a certain amount of protein, and a handful of other nutrients.[3] So when a village asks for bread, rice, or wheat, those requests can be converted into requests for carbohydrates. And when a village asks for beef or beans, those requests can be converted into requests for protein and iron. So by simply abstracting the task from ‚ÄúHow best can we meet all these food requests?‚Äù to ‚ÄúHow best can we meet the nutritional needs indicated by these requests?‚Äù the WFP was able to dramatically reduce the number of constraints being imposed on the logistical optimization problem WFP needed to solve, making its task far simpler.

How far should you abstract things? To the level that feels most useful. Yes, in the private sector you can abstract almost any problem to ‚Äúhow do we maximize the lifetime discounted profits of the company,‚Äù but I don‚Äôt think it would be controversial to say that that formulation is not particularly useful if you‚Äôre trying to pick the best box sizes for your company to stock for online orders. But there‚Äôs also no real cost to thinking about your problem at that level of generality for a little while, so my advice would be: if you haven‚Äôt gotten to a level of abstraction that feels silly, you probably haven‚Äôt abstracted enough.

3. Let Your Articulation Change#

My last suggestion is to allow your understanding of the problem you are trying to solve evolve. As your project develops, you will learn new things about your problem context, and you should allow those to inform how you are thinking of your problem. This is true not only for you ‚Äî the data scientist ‚Äî but also your stakeholder. We‚Äôll discuss stakeholder management more in our next chapter, but you should regularly be asking them: ‚Äúdoes the way we‚Äôve articulated the problem we‚Äôre trying to solve still feel right to you given everything we‚Äôve learned?‚Äù

The Role of Domain Expertise#

Application is, in many ways, what sets data science apart from the disciplines of mathematics, computer science, and statistics. And to apply the tools of data science tools effectively, by definition, requires an understanding of the domain to which your tools are being deployed. This fact makes many data scientists uncomfortable ‚Äî after all, many young data scientists do not even know the industry in which they will be employed when the graduate, never mind feel they can lay claim to being a ‚Äúdomain expert‚Äù in any specific substantive field.

A consequence of this discomfort is that ‚Äúdomain expertise‚Äù is that the term ‚Äúdomain expertise‚Äù is often invoked as a way of abdicating responsibility for part of an analysis as ‚Äúsomebody else‚Äôs problem‚Äù (a dangerously powerful construction, as explained by the incomparable Douglas Adams).

But it would be a mistake to view domain expertise as beyond the responsibility of the data scientist for two reasons. First, engaging with the details of a substantive domain to tailor the application of data science methods to solve a specific problem isn‚Äôt ancillary to the job of a data scientists ‚Äî it‚Äôs a data scientist‚Äôs comparative advantage. Data scientists (generally) are not the most technically skilled mathematicians, statisticians, or programmers ‚Äî we are professionals who specialize in taking the best insights from all three of these skills sets and adapting them to fit the specific needs of a specific problem. So embrace the role of ‚Äúdomain knowledge!‚Äù

The second reason is that while you are unlikely to be a ‚Äúdomain expert‚Äù yourself, learning to solicit important information about a domain from true domain experts is a skill unto itself. As we will detail in our readings on ‚Äústakeholder management,‚Äù stakeholders may be experts in their particular substantive domain, but because they (usually) won‚Äôt understand data science, they are unlikely to ever provide you with the domain knowledge you need to do your job successfully (and you can‚Äôt get away with just saying ‚Äúyes, this project failed, but it was because you didn‚Äôt tell me X!‚Äù See the discussion above of the single criterion used to evaluate data scientists). So learning to partner with your stakeholder and domain experts to understand a problem is a key part of understanding it properly, and thus eventually solving it.

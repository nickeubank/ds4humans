Matching Exercise#

You have been hired by a mortgage firm (a company that both issues and collects mortgage payments from homeowners). This servicing firm is thinking about entering the market for ‚Äúno money down‚Äù mortgages ‚Äî mortgages in which the buyer does not have to pay a large percentage of the value of the house when they take out the loan (in the US, most people pay 20% of the value of a house up front when taking out a mortgage). Instead, they just pay a slightly higher interest rate.

Before they start offering this product, though, they want to know the effect this option will have on the likelihood people who take out a mortgage from them will make their payments. In other words, they want to know:

What is the effect of a mortgage being issued as a ‚Äúno money down‚Äù mortgage on the likelihood that the mortgage will enter delinquency (the homeowner will be at least 30 days late on a mortgage payment) during the first two years of the mortgage?

On the one hand, we might expect that someone taking out a ‚Äúno money down‚Äù mortgage would be less likely to enter delinquency because they didn‚Äôt have to put a lot of cash into a down payment, making it easier for them to afford their payments.

On the other hand, the kind of people who can put 20% down on a house are the kind of people who are able to save effectively, so someone who opts for a ‚Äúno money down‚Äù mortgage with a higher interest rate (instead of saving to pay the 20% down payment) may be more likely to also struggle to make their regular payments.

Data#

To answer this question, you have been given REAL data on a sample of all US Standard single family home mortgages purchased or insured by Freddie Mac. This is a random sample of standard mortgages issued in the calendar year 2004.

Original Loan-to-Value (LTV) is the share of the original value of the home that was borrowed. A value of 100 means there was no down payment (‚Äúno money down‚Äù), while higher values indicate a down payment was made.

From this point forward, when I say ‚Äúno-money-down mortgages,‚Äù I mean 95% LTV mortgages.

To keep things simple, let‚Äôs compare these (almost) no money down mortgages to mortgages with a 20% down payment (LTV value of 80). This is the modal mortgage in the US.

Matching!#

OK, let‚Äôs finally do some matching!

To do our matching, we will be using pymatchit, a port of the exceptionally good MatchIt which is only natively available in R. It supports genetic matching, exact matching, coarsened exact matching, nearest neighbor matching, subclass matching, cardinality matching, and more..

This library was just recently created (and I have to imagine with significant LLM help), so it‚Äôs a little early to be using it. With that said, matching is one area where R packages (namely MatchIt) are still well ahead of Python libraries, and after looking things over pretty carefully it seems well implemented ‚Äî it has a reasonable test suite, includes diagnostic plots (which are something that‚Äôs so often missing from Python matching and are great for learning), and testing the matching algorithm we‚Äôre using (coarse exact matching) things seem to match between R and this package. So let‚Äôs try it. :)

So install it with pip install pymatchit-causal (not pip install pymatchit), and quickly review the README for the repo here.

Exercise 8#

Let‚Äôs do some coarsened exact matching.

In particular, we will match on credit_score, original_interest_rate, and first_time_homebuyer_flag.

Recall that with coarsened exact matching, we have to specify how continuous variables (like credit_score and original_interest_rate) should be coarsened. This is accomplished with the cutpoints keyword. Note the cutpoints takes the upper and lower values to be bounds ‚Äî so [1, 2, 3] will create two groups: between 1 and 2 and between 2 and 3. Values below 1 and above 3 will be dropped.

from pymatchit import MatchIt

mortgages["first_time"] = (mortgages["first_time_homebuyer_flag"] == "Y").astype("int")

# Deterministic sort to ensure
# random seed generates same result
mortgages = mortgages.sort_values("loan_sequence_number")

m = MatchIt(
    data=mortgages,
    method="cem",
    replace=False,
    random_state=42,
    estimand="ATE",
    cutpoints={
        "credit_score": [500, 575, 650, 675, 700, 720, 740, 760, 780, 800],
        "original_interest_rate": [4.25, 5.25, 6.25, 7.25, 8.25],
    },
)


# Format: treatment_variable ~ covariate1 + covariate2 + ...
m.fit("no_money_down ~ credit_score + original_interest_rate " "+ first_time")

Exercise 12#

Using statsmodels, fit a weighted regression using the data in m.matched_data with just no_money_down as a regressor. Store the resulting coefficient in "ex12_bivariate_effect".

Note that pymatchit can provide two different types of weights ‚Äî weights for estimating ATE, and weights for estimating ATT.

To estimate ATT, the weight of a treated (here, no_money_down) observation will always be 1, and the weight for a control observation will be the number of treatment observations in the coarse match group divided by the number of controls.

To estimate ATE, the weights are just the total observations in the coarse match group divided by number of treatment observations (for treated observations) and total observations in the coarse match group divided by the number of control observations (for control observations).

Here, we‚Äôve specified the estimate is "ATE", so we get weights for estimating ATE.

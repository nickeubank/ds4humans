\begin{frame}[c]{P-Values}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/resume_experiment_regression_table.png}

\alert{What is the probability that the *true* effect} of reporting computer skills on your resume \alert{is 0} and that the difference we see between resumes with and without reported computer skills is the result of chance variation?
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[c]{P-Values}

Want to know: $Pr(H_0 \text{ is true})$\\[1ex]
where $H_0$ is our Null Hypothesis of zero effect. 

\pause Or, to be more precise, we want to know:

$\text{what we want to know: } Pr(H_0 \text{ is true}|\text{Observed Data})$

\pause And doesn't that *feel* like what a p-value is?

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[c]{P-Values}
But p-value \alert{$\neq$} $Pr(H_0 \text{ is true}|\text{Observed Data})$ \\[2ex]

\pause p-value \alert{$\equiv$} $Pr(\text{Observed Data}|H_0 \text{ is true})$ \\[1ex]
\pause probability we would observe a coefficient at least this large \emph{in a world where the Null Hypothesis is true}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[c]{P-Values}

How do $Pr(\text{Observed Data}| H_0 \text{ is true})$ and $Pr(H_0 \text{ is true}|\text{Observed Data})$ relate? \\[1ex]

\pause The answer is given by Bayes Rule:

$$Pr(A|B) = \frac{Pr(B|A)Pr(A)}{Pr(B)}$$

\pause 
Fudging a little:

$$Pr(H_0 \text{ is true}|\text{Data}) = \frac{\alert{Pr(\text{Data}| H_0 \text{is true})} * Pr(H_0 \text{is true})}{Pr(\text{Data})}$$

\end{frame}
